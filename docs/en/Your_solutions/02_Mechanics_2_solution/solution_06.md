# 6. Energy Dissipation

## Problem Statement
A tennis ball is dropped from a height of $2.0$ m. After each bounce, it loses 30% of its mechanical energy. To what height does it rise after the second bounce?

## Step-by-Step Solution

1. **Understand the initial energy:**
   Let the initial height be $h_0 = 2.0 \text{ m}$. The initial potential energy is $E_0 = mgh_0$.
2. **Energy after the first bounce:**
   The ball loses 30% of its energy upon bouncing, meaning it retains 70% of its mechanical energy.
   $$ E_1 = 0.70 \times E_0 $$
   The height after the first bounce, $h_1$, corresponds to this new energy:
   $$ mgh_1 = 0.70 mgh_0 \implies h_1 = 0.70 h_0 $$
3. **Energy after the second bounce:**
   The ball loses another 30% of its energy upon the second bounce, retaining 70% of the energy it had before the second bounce.
   $$ E_2 = 0.70 \times E_1 = 0.70 \times (0.70 \times E_0) = 0.49 \times E_0 $$
   The height after the second bounce, $h_2$, corresponds to this energy:
   $$ mgh_2 = 0.49 mgh_0 \implies h_2 = 0.49 h_0 $$
4. **Calculate final height:**
   Substitute the initial height $h_0 = 2.0 \text{ m}$:
   $$ h_2 = 0.49 \times 2.0 = 0.98 \text{ m} $$
5. **Conclusion:**
   The ball rises to a height of **0.98 m** after the second bounce.
