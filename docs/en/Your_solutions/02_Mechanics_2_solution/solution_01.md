# 1. Gravitational Dependence

## Problem Statement
A simple pendulum has a period of 4 seconds on Earth. What would its period be on the Moon, where the gravitational acceleration is about 1/6th of Earth's?

What is the required length of a simple pendulum to have a period of exactly 1 second on Earth?

## Step-by-Step Solution

**Part 1: Period on the Moon**

1. The period of a simple pendulum is given by the formula:
   $$ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} $$
   where $L$ is the length and $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity.

2. We are given the period on Earth $T_E = 4\text{ s}$. Therefore:
   $$ T_E = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_E}} = 4\text{ s} $$

3. For the Moon, the gravitational acceleration is $g_M = \frac{g_E}{6}$. The period on the Moon $T_M$ will be:
   $$ T_M = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_M}} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_E/6}} = \sqrt{6} \left( 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_E}} \right) $$

4. Substitute $T_E$ into the equation:
   $$ T_M = \sqrt{6} \, T_E = \sqrt{6} \times 4\text{ s} \approx 2.449 \times 4 \approx 9.796\text{ s} $$

**Part 2: Required length for a 1-second period on Earth**

1. Using the same formula $T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$, set $T = 1\text{ s}$ and solve for $L$.

2. Note that $g$ on Earth is approximately $9.81\text{ m/s}^2$.

3. Rearranging the formula for $L$:
   $$ L = \frac{T^2 \cdot g}{4\pi^2} $$

4. Substitute the values:
   $$ L = \frac{(1\text{ s})^2 \cdot 9.81\text{ m/s}^2}{4\pi^2} \approx \frac{9.81}{39.478} \approx 0.248\text{ m} \text{ or } 24.8\text{ cm} $$
